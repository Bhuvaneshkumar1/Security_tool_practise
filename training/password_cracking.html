CREDENTIAL ATTACKS

How accounts are compromised — and how organizations prevent it

1. What Is Password Cracking?

Password cracking is the process of recovering or abusing credentials to gain unauthorized access to systems. It is responsible for some of the **largest financial and data losses in history**.

  • Online attacks (login attempts)
  • Offline attacks (cracked hashes)
  • Credential reuse across services

2. How Attackers Take Over Accounts

  • Reuse leaked passwords from previous breaches
  • Target login pages with automation
  • Exploit missing rate limits or MFA
  • Crack weak password hashes offline

Most attacks do not guess passwords — they reuse them.

3. Real-World Breach Timeline

2012 — LinkedIn
117 million passwords leaked (unsalted SHA-1).
2013 — Adobe
153 million accounts compromised due to reversible encryption.
2016 — Dropbox
Credential reuse from earlier breach enabled access.
2019 — Capital One
Weak access controls led to credential exposure.
2021 — Colonial Pipeline
Single reused password with no MFA caused shutdown.

4. What Failed vs What Should Have Been Done

Incident What Failed What Should Have Been Done
LinkedIn Fast, unsalted hashes bcrypt / Argon2 + salting
Adobe Reversible encryption One-way hashing only
Dropbox Password reuse Password rotation + MFA
Capital One Over-permissive access Least-privilege IAM
Colonial Pipeline No MFA Mandatory MFA everywhere

5. Why Password Attacks Still Work

  • Humans reuse passwords
  • Organizations underestimate credential risk
  • MFA adoption is incomplete
  • Weak hashing still exists
